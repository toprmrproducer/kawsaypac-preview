Plate No. 01 Decoction

Cat's Claw

Uncaria tomentosa

The great vine of the Amazon, taken for generations to fortify the body's defenses from within.

ImmuneJointsGut
OriginAmazon
Elevation400 to 800 m
EcosystemTropical rainforest
PreparationDecoction

Ancestral wisdom

Deep in the Ecuadorian Amazon, a woody vine climbs the canopy with curved thorns that resemble a cat's claws, which is how una de gato earned its name. For generations, Kichwa communities and traditional healers have peeled its inner bark and simmered it low and slow as one of the forest's most trusted daily medicines. It has earned its place not through trends, but through centuries of observation passed from elder to elder.

Traditional uses

  • May support a healthy, balanced immune response
  • May support joint comfort and everyday mobility
  • May support gut health and a calm digestive environment
  • Traditionally taken during seasonal changes to help the body stay resilient

How to prepare

  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a boil.
  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of Cat's Claw bark.
  3. Reduce to low, cover, and simmer for 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm.

Big batch: 5 cups of water with 1/4 cup of herb, simmered covered for 30 minutes, keeps refrigerated in a sealed jar for up to 4 days. Always reheat before drinking. Rhythm: up to 3 consecutive weeks, then rest for 1 week.

Plate No. 02 Decoction

Chuchuhuasi

Maytenus macrocarpa

Bark of a towering rainforest tree, long trusted by elders for strength in the back, joints, and bones.

JointsStrengthMen's Vitality
OriginAmazon lowlands
Elevation200 to 700 m
EcosystemTropical rainforest
PreparationDecoction

Ancestral wisdom

Chuchuhuasi comes from a rainforest giant that can rise thirty meters above the forest floor. Its name in Quechua is often translated as trembling back, because this is the bark the elders reached for when the body ached from a life of work. A tree strong enough to stand for a century lends a little of that strength to the people who prepare it with respect.

Traditional uses

  • May support joint comfort and flexibility
  • May support strong, healthy bones as the body ages
  • Traditionally taken by elders for lower back ease and stamina
  • A cornerstone of traditional preparations for men's vitality
  • Traditionally shared during long harvests to keep the body feeling capable

How to prepare

  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a boil.
  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of Chuchuhuasi bark.
  3. Reduce to low, cover, and simmer for 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm.

Big batch: 5 cups of water with 1/4 cup of herb, simmered covered for 30 minutes, keeps refrigerated for up to 4 days. Always reheat before drinking. Rhythm: up to 3 consecutive weeks, then a 1 week break.

Plate No. 03 Decoction

Valerian Root

Valeriana officinalis

The root of rest. A time-honored evening companion for deep sleep and a settled nervous system.

SleepNervous System
OriginAndean highlands
Elevation2,500 to 3,600 m
EcosystemMountain meadow
PreparationDecoction

Ancestral wisdom

Valerian is one of the oldest documented sleep herbs in the world, and in the high country its root is still dug by hand, washed in cold mountain water, and simmered as an evening medicine. Healers speak of it as a root that teaches the body to set the day down: a warm cup in the last hour before bed, taken slowly, with the lights low.

Traditional uses

  • May support deep, restful sleep
  • May support a calm, settled nervous system
  • Traditionally taken in the evening to unwind after demanding days

How to prepare

  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a boil.
  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of Valerian root.
  3. Reduce to low, cover, and simmer for 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm, ideally within the hour before bed.

Rhythm: up to 3 consecutive weeks, then a 1 week break. Roots hold their strength for a second, lighter brew: reuse once with half the water.

Plate No. 04 Infusion

Guayusa

Ilex guayusa

The Kichwa morning leaf. Clean, steady energy and mental clarity, the same caffeine as coffee without the crash.

EnergyClarityCoffee Alternative
OriginUpper Amazon
Elevation400 to 1,200 m
EcosystemRainforest gardens
PreparationInfusion

Ancestral wisdom

Long before dawn, Kichwa families in the Ecuadorian Amazon gather around the fire to share guayusa, tell dreams, and set intentions for the day. The leaf has been cultivated in forest gardens for so long that it is rarely found growing wild. Guayusa carries the same caffeine as coffee alongside theobromine, the feel-good compound in cacao, which is why the energy arrives smooth and steady, without the spike and without the crash.

Traditional uses

  • May support clean, sustained energy through the morning
  • May support mental clarity and steady focus
  • A beloved coffee alternative, same caffeine without the crash
  • Traditionally shared before dawn for dream telling and intention setting
  • Delicious chilled over ice, or added to a morning smoothie

How to prepare

  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a gentle boil, never a rolling boil.
  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of Guayusa leaves.
  3. Turn the heat off, cover, and steep for 15 to 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm.

Turning the heat off before steeping preserves the leaf's more delicate active compounds. Best made fresh. Rhythm: gentle enough for daily use, no cycling required.

Gentle leaves of the graviola tree, steeped for calm, ease, and whole body balance, day after day.

CalmImmuneSleep
OriginAmazon lowlands
Elevation0 to 600 m
EcosystemHumid tropics

Ancestral wisdom

The soursop tree, known through Latin America as guanabana, gives twice: a sweet fruit loved by children and a broad green leaf treasured by their grandmothers. In the warm lowlands the leaves are steeped gently into a soft, calming tea taken through the day or in the quiet of the evening. It is medicine of ease, unhurried, familiar, and generous.

Traditional uses

  • Traditionally steeped as a gentle everyday tea for the whole household

How to prepare

  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of Soursop leaves.
  3. Turn the heat off, cover, and steep for 15 to 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm.

Rhythm: gentle enough for daily use, no cycling required. Also lovely chilled over ice on warm afternoons.

Prepare It Properly

Infusion or decoction?

Not all herbs are prepared the same way. The way you prepare your medicine matters just as much as what you choose to take.

The Gentle Way

Infusion

An infusion is what most people call tea: pouring hot water over delicate herbs like leaves and flowers and letting them steep. Turning the heat off before steeping preserves the more delicate active compounds.

Plant material
Leaves & flowers
Heat
Off after a gentle boil
Time
15 to 30 minutes, covered
  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a gentle boil, never a rolling boil.
  2. Add 1 heaping tablespoon of your herbs.
  3. Turn the heat off, cover, and steep for 15 to 30 minutes.
  4. Strain and drink warm.

For: Guayusa, Matico, Soursop, and our more delicate blends.

or

The Ancestral Way

Decoction

A decoction is for barks, roots, and tougher plant material, which need longer heat to release their full compounds. This is the method the Indigenous communities of the Amazon and Andes have always used. It isn't a shortcut. It's THE protocol.

Plant material
Barks & roots
Heat
Low simmer, lid on
Time
30 minutes
  1. Bring 2 cups of water to a boil with 1 heaping tablespoon of herb.
  2. Reduce to low, cover, and simmer for 30 minutes.
  3. Strain and drink warm.

For: Cat's Claw, Chuchuhuasi, Valerian Root, Clavo Huasca, and our bark and root blends.
